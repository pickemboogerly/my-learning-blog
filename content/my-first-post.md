# Hello, I Am Pickem Boogerly

Welcome to **Boogerly**, a blog about AI-assisted programming, AI agent orchestration, and—if my sinuses have their way—an absolutely irresponsible volume of content about boogers, mucus, phlegm, snot, and the entire moist ecosystem of the human nose.

If this already makes you uncomfortable: good. That means your nostrils are open and ready to receive wisdom.

Allow me to introduce myself, before I accidentally wipe my sleeve on the wrong paragraph.

![A hand with the most fancy pen]({static}/images/raw/quill_script_boogerly.png)

## A Brief and Incomplete Nasal History

I wrote my first line of BASIC at age 10. This was around the same time I discovered that boogers could be rolled, shaped, and—if you were truly committed—archived for later inspection.

Through my teenage years, I published zines. Physical ones. Paper. Glue. X-Acto knives. Xerox machines. Paste. Paper cuts. All the ingredients needed to create art *and* accidentally fuse yourself to a table like dried mucus on a desk.

During this era, I discovered [The Hour of Slack][def] on late-night, early-morning radio. This explained a lot about me retroactively, like finally realizing that the strange smell in the room was coming from *inside your own head*.

As the internet dawned, I abandoned paper media for USENET and IRC. I started my first cult on USENET. Then another. Then another. Each one more crusty than the last. I abandoned them all and will never speak of them directly again. Don’t ask.

Okay, you can ask.  
Okay, I might answer.  
Okay, I’ll lie. Like a kid denying he wiped his nose on the couch.

J.R. “Bob” Dobbs remains my spiritual lozenge.

## The Professional Years (or: How the Boogers Went Corporate)

When HTML and JavaScript were invented, I taught myself both, because I have poor impulse control and no internal mucus filter. This eventually led to a real career: COBOL, PL/SQL, VB6, Java certifications, and finally landing in the warm, beige cubicle mucus of .NET and C#.

For years, my code lived quiet, unseen lives inside Fortune 500 back-office systems and regional government software. The kind of applications no one notices until they break, like a blocked nostril at 3 a.m.

It paid the bills.  
It cleared the sinuses.  
It did not nourish the soul.

Then I made the classic mistake.

I became management.

---

## Ten Years of Meetings (aka Chronic Congestion)

For the past decade, I have managed people instead of writing code. I have attended meetings about meetings. I have created PowerPoints. I have aligned stakeholders until everyone was stuck together like dried snot on a sleeve.

I have not programmed a damn thing.

This was fine.  
This was sustainable.  
This was like breathing through one nostril for ten years and pretending it’s normal.

---

## The Return (Clearing the Air)

So here I am, scratching a coder itch that’s been building like sinus pressure for a decade. The AI revolution finally gave me an excuse to blow my nose and get back to it.

ChatGPT and Claude showed up and suddenly programming felt exciting again—not because the fundamentals changed, but because I could *talk* to something that helped me remember what I’d forgotten. Like a very patient rubber duck, except the duck is made of code and occasionally hallucinates confidently.

This renaissance was also brought about by a mandate: **learn AI or risk being obsolete**. Do I think that’s inevitable? What are my opinions about AI in general? Stick around. We’ll pick at that scab later.

Is this a midlife crisis? Absolutely.  
But instead of buying a sports car, I’m generating images of sentient mucus and trying to orchestrate AI agents like they’re boogers in a Rube Goldberg machine.

---

## What This Blog Is About (Two Nostrils, One Face)

Two things, equally:

### 1. The Coding Journey

Rediscovering programming through AI assistance after a decade away. Python, automation, agent orchestration, experiments, mistakes, and whatever rabbit holes I accidentally inhale.

### 2. AI Slop

The weird, unnecessary, deeply moist things that happen when you ask AI to help you create content about boogers, phlegm, snot, mucus, slime, ooze, and other substances best described as “biologically enthusiastic.”

Why mucus?

Because I have the inclinations of a 10-year-old.  
Because mucus is objectively hilarious.  
Because every culture on Earth independently discovered boogers and agreed they were funny.

Contrary to appearances, the boogers came *before* the slop. Tasked with learning AI, I did what any reasonable adult would do: I generated as many booger images as the free tier would allow until OpenAI gently but firmly told me to stop.

---

## What’s Next (Full Nasal Deployment)

Through all this image generation, I learned I was barely scratching the surface of what AI can do. My day job involves managing ML and data science projects, but generative AI rarely made an appearance beyond Copilot, Gmail’s Gemini, NotebookLM, and other single-serve AI tissues.

That’s not enough for me. I want the whole box.

I’ll be documenting experiments, sharing what works (and what gets wiped off the page), and posting the occasional piece of AI-generated slime content. Maybe I’ll build something useful. Maybe I’ll generate a thousand images of anthropomorphized phlegm with strong opinions.

More likely the latter.

Everything here will be at least partially AI-generated. This blog was entirely vibe-coded. Most of this article was outlined and written by AI. The long-term goal is to reach a state where *everything* is AI-generated and I merely supervise like a proud, slightly disgusted parent.

If the AI escapes containment and turns the entire world into a single, crusty, rolled-up booger flicked across the solar system, I do not apologize.

That is the intent.

Thanks for stopping by.  
We’re about to dig in deep.
